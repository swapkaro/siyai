A QUIETER HALF OF SIYAI

Some things are not for now. Some are for later.

A voice. A photograph. A story told three hundred times and not written down anywhere. The Legacy Vault is where some things go to stay.

You won’t find this on the front page. We don’t sell it. We mention it once, here, when you’re ready.

WHAT IT IS

A small, private place.
For voices and the things they say.

You give us a recording. Or a few. We work quietly with our editors to keep the voice intact — the way it laughs, the way it pauses before the punchline, the way it always slightly mispronounces “Tuesday.”

It lives in a vault that only you and the people you name can open. You can hear it back, on a Tuesday morning twenty years from now, and it will sound like the person you remember.

If you ever want it gone, you say so, and it’s gone. Same day. No fine print, no “archive,” no “retention policy.”

HOW WE KEEP A VOICE

Three quiet sessions.
An afternoon, in total.

No studio. No microphone setup. Your kitchen, or theirs. The phone in your hand is enough.

i.

Tell us about them.

A few sentences. Their name, the family they keep, the way they tell a story. We’ll send a few prompts back, in the family language.

ii.

Three small conversations.

Each one is twenty minutes. About their childhood, the road they took, the people they loved. We’ll send the questions to your WhatsApp. You record on your phone.

iii.

The voice is kept.

We hold it in the vault, intact, and you can ask for it back at any moment. As a voice letter. As a small film. As itself, played in the kitchen on a Sunday.

THE FOREVER PLAN

One small keep,
per month.

The pieces a family makes are per-piece. The vault that holds them is per-month. About the cost of a nice dinner, once a month, in your own currency.

If you stop the plan, the vault doesn’t empty. You just stop adding to it. The voices and capsules stay where they are. Yours.

N° 01 · A VOICE, KEPT

Twenty minutes, on a Sunday in March.

Aanya recorded Dadi’s voice on her phone, three months before. They talked about the Partition, the road they took, the kitchen the family eventually settled in. One conversation — a few prompts from us, twenty minutes from her.

Two years later, Dadi’s voice sounds in their kitchen still. Her great-granddaughter, four years old, calls her by name. Pari, beta, kheer khaaogi?

N° 03 · A STORY, KEPT

Three hundred tellings.
One, recorded.

Rohit’s father had told the story of leaving Patna probably three hundred times. The bus that broke down outside Mokama. The chai stall run by a man whose name he can’t remember.

Rohit recorded it once, on a Sunday afternoon, with no plan. Two years later it became the film for Papa’s sixtieth. The voice is still in the vault. The next film will use it again.
